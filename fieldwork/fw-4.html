Play to Learn: Making National Education Fun through Game-based Learning

Date: 2025-09-18 Location: Republic Polytechnic Method: Workshop Attendance & Observation

Summary

This workshop explored how serious games can be used to make complex educational topics, especially National Education, more engaging and accessible to learners. A key takeaway was that games are effective not simply because they are fun, but because their mechanics can translate abstract systems, tensions, and trade-offs into something that players can actively experience. The session also stressed that gameplay alone is not enough: post-game reflection is essential in helping learners deconstruct their experience, identify what they have learnt, and connect it back to the intended learning outcomes. Another important point was that physical games often perform better than digital ones in these settings, both because they are easier to prototype and because they make learner behaviour more visible and easier to observe.

Observations

  • Serious games can be implemented to tackle difficult or complex educational subjects, and they appear to produce positive outcomes when the learning goals are carefully aligned with the gameplay.
  • Game mechanics can translate different aspects of a theme into an experiential system, allowing learners to encounter issues through action rather than only through explanation.
  • Play and imagination are important because they make learning more engaging, support problem-solving and creative thinking, and help move learning beyond traditional broadcast-style education.
  • Games create a safe environment for failure, experimentation, and decision-making. This is especially useful when the subject matter involves risk, trade-offs, or complex systems that would be difficult to simulate in real life.
  • Because gameplay is observable, it can later support conversations about what decisions players made, what they avoided doing, and how different actions shaped different outcomes.
  • The workshop emphasised that rules, win conditions, objectives, and feedback systems are not just structural tools for the game, but also tools for measuring learner performance and shaping player behaviour.
  • Physical games were framed as especially effective because they are easier to test, cheaper to run, and make interaction more social and more visible than digital games, where distraction can be higher.
  • One repeated point was that games are not the answer to everything. They are useful when the learning outcomes can genuinely be translated into mechanics, but not all content or assessment goals are best served through play.

Challenges & Reflections

One of the most useful insights from this workshop was that engagement alone is not the same as learning. While games can motivate learners and help them retain experiences more vividly, the workshop repeatedly stressed the need for post-game reflection in order to surface actual learning outcomes. This is especially important because every player’s experience of a game will be different: different strategies, biases, restrictions, and interactions can all shape what the learner takes away. Another challenge discussed was alignment. If the learning outcomes are not clearly embedded into the core mechanics, then the game risks becoming entertaining but educationally weak. This reminded me that game-based learning is not just about making serious content “fun,” but about designing systems where the act of play itself produces the intended reflection, trade-offs, or insights.

I was also interested in the workshop’s framing of National Education as something that often carries a stigma of being boring, irrelevant, or inaccessible to students. The proposed solution was not to simplify the topic into something shallow, but to use game systems to make the complexity more approachable and relatable. This connects strongly to my own research, as I am also dealing with difficult or emotionally loaded social themes and trying to make them accessible through play. The emphasis on physical games was particularly useful to me, since it reinforced my own suspicion that analogue formats can sometimes better support visible interaction, observation, and post-play discussion than fully digital systems.

Next Steps

  • Consider more carefully how learning outcomes can be translated directly into core mechanics rather than being added on top of the gameplay afterward.
  • Explore how post-game reflection can be embedded more intentionally into my own prototypes, especially when the learning or emotional outcome is subtle and cannot be assumed from play alone.
  • Think more about what makes a good “hook” in a game and how moments of excitement, challenge, or scarcity keep players invested in the system.
  • Reflect on the strengths of physical games for testing and observation, particularly in relation to communication, body language, and visible decision-making.
  • Investigate how complex social or cultural topics can be made more relatable to learners without flattening the nuance of the subject matter.
