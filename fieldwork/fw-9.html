Fieldwork

Research Residency at the Mixed Reality Lab, ADM NTU

Date: 2025-12-01 - 2025-12-12 Location: ADM, NTU Method: Research Assistantship, Observation & Interview

Summary

During my two-week research residency, I volunteered as a research assistant at the Mixed Reality Lab at ADM, NTU. My role involved participating in ongoing research into virtual heritage and the emerging technologies used to support preservation work. I was able to gain insight into the research process through conversations with lead researchers, observing how projects are planned and executed, and supporting parts of the post-production workflow to understand how preservation outputs are edited, assembled, and eventually showcased to audiences.

Observations

  • Virtual heritage research often focuses on preserving sites that may soon be altered, demolished, or otherwise lost, whether those sites are natural or built environments.
  • A major priority in this work is capturing the site as completely as possible so that future audiences can still explore it in immersive ways.
  • Emerging technologies such as 360 cameras are useful because they can record spatial experience more richly than conventional photography or video.
  • However, the process does not end with data capture. A significant amount of labour happens in post-production, where the raw footage and artefacts are processed, edited, and arranged into a form that can be meaningfully presented.
  • Interviews and informal conversations with researchers revealed that heritage preservation is not only a technical task, but also a curatorial and interpretive one.
  • While visual accuracy is an important goal, it is not necessarily enough to sustain audience interest on its own.
  • In order for audiences to feel invested in a preserved site, there often needs to be a storytelling layer that creates emotional connection, context, or relevance.

Challenges & Reflections

One of the most useful things I learnt from this assistantship is that preservation alone does not automatically produce engagement. A site can be documented with great technical accuracy and still feel distant or dry to an audience if there is no narrative or interpretive structure helping them understand why it matters. This was particularly interesting to me because it suggests that virtual heritage is not just about recording what exists, but also about shaping how people encounter and emotionally process that preserved material.

This also helped me think more critically about the role of researchers and designers in heritage work. In many cases, they are not simply neutral recorders. They also have to make decisions about how to frame, edit, and present material in order to make it accessible and compelling. That may involve introducing a storytelling element, contextual information, or even taking careful creative liberties so that the preserved output does more than function as a static archive.

This experience connects directly to my own research interests. Even though my project has shifted away from heritage preservation in a conventional sense, I am still concerned with how meaningful stories, memories, and lived experiences can be translated into designed forms that people want to engage with. This assistantship reinforced the idea that preservation without emotional or narrative connection can easily become inert, and that design has a role in helping audiences care.

Next Steps

  • Continue exploring the relationship between documentation and storytelling, especially how preserved materials can be framed to support emotional connection.
  • Reflect further on the role of post-production as a design and interpretive stage rather than a merely technical one.
  • Consider how immersive technologies might support my own research, not as spectacle, but as tools for shaping presence and engagement.
  • Think more critically about when creative liberties are necessary in order to help audiences connect with preserved content.
  • Investigate how researchers in adjacent fields combine factual capture with narrative framing in order to make complex materials accessible.
