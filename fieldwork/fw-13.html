Fieldwork

User Testing Session 3 - Three-Generation Family

Date: 2026-06-17 Location: Ho Chi Minh City Method: Prototype User Testing & Observation

Summary

For the third user testing session, I worked with a family that included all three generations: two paternal grandparents, two parents, and a son. This made it the richest intergenerational composition I had tested so far, but also the most emotionally difficult. While the grandparents and parents were open to the game and participated quite enthusiastically, the grandson was extremely quiet throughout the session. He skipped most of the questions, only answered when other family members nudged him or partially supplied the answer for him, and generally responded with only one line when he did speak. This created a lot of tension in the room and extended the game session significantly, because the rest of the family kept waiting for him and encouraging him to answer while he remained silent.

Observations

  • This session revealed a much more difficult and less ideal communication dynamic than the previous two, particularly centred around the grandson’s refusal or inability to participate openly.
  • The grandson repeatedly switched questions, gave only minimal responses, and did not explain why he did not want to answer, which made the tension itself a central part of the session.
  • Despite this, the rest of the family still showed a strong desire for him to communicate, which suggests that the lack of response did not come from indifference on their part, but perhaps from a more complex relational dynamic.
  • The parents and grandparents, by contrast, were open and highly detailed in their storytelling, especially when speaking about their youth.
  • A major recurring theme across their stories was poverty, improvisation, and the creative ways they had to manage daily life with limited resources.
  • They also expressed fondness for older objects, tools, and routines that were difficult to use but emotionally significant because they were closely tied to shared hardship and memory.
  • At the same time, all generations acknowledged that while technological progress has made life easier, it has also reduced opportunities for collective in-person activities and human connection.
  • The family mentioned examples such as watching television together in the past or playing physical games together, in contrast to the more isolated and digital nature of social life now.
  • Even with the grandson’s lack of communication, the family still gave positive feedback about the experience, including the repeated observation that they learned stories they would not otherwise have heard.
  • An especially interesting dynamic was that the mother, as the daughter-in-law of the grandparents, also learned many stories from them for the first time.
  • Even more strikingly, the grandfather remarked that he learned new things about the grandmother’s youth from her storytelling during the session, despite the fact that they had been married for a long time.

Challenges & Reflections

This was probably the most emotionally difficult but also the most revealing session. The grandson’s silence made it impossible to treat the prototype as a neat conversational success, but that is precisely why the session is important. It exposed the reality that in some families, the issue is not only that people do not know what to ask, but that there may already be tension, withdrawal, or emotional barriers that make answering itself difficult. In that sense, the session reflects the exact kind of relational challenge that this research is trying to address.

At the same time, the rest of the family’s participation showed that the desire for connection was still present. The parents and grandparents kept encouraging the grandson, which suggests that even in a strained dynamic, there is still a wish for communication and closeness. This is important, because it means the absence of speech is not necessarily the absence of care. The session also showed that the prototypes can still generate valuable storytelling even when one member is resistant, and that useful insights can emerge from the tension itself.

Thematically, the stories shared in this session were very rich. The repeated discussion of poverty, hardship, improvisation, and changing technological conditions created a powerful intergenerational contrast between older and younger life experiences. What I found especially interesting is that the family did not frame the past only as hardship, but also as a time of stronger human connection. This suggests that the project is not simply about nostalgia, but about how material conditions, social structures, and technology shape the way families spend time together and remember one another.

Finally, the fact that even long-married partners and in-laws learned new stories from one another reinforced a key idea in my project: there are always more stories to be told, even in relationships that seem already well known. This was one of the clearest demonstrations that familial storytelling is never really “finished.”

Next Steps

  • Reflect more carefully on how the prototypes function in families with difficult or tense communication styles, rather than only in smoother testing environments.
  • Consider whether some sessions need more built-in scaffolding for quieter or more reluctant participants.
  • Continue investigating how themes of poverty, technological change, and shifting social connection recur across generations.
  • Recognise that tension during a session is itself a form of data, not simply a failed outcome.
  • Explore how to design for families where not all participants are equally ready or willing to speak, while still preserving the potential for stories to emerge.
