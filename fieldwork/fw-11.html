User Testing Session 1 - Family with Married Daughter

Date: [Insert date] Location: [Insert location] Method: Prototype User Testing & Observation

Summary

For this first user testing session, I worked with a family consisting of a father, a mother, a daughter who had recently gotten married and moved out, and a son who was still in university. The daughter’s husband was initially meant to join as well, but he could not make it at the last minute, which later turned out to have shaped the experience of the session quite significantly. At first, the father was reluctant to participate, which was actually a recurring pattern among the families I contacted: the mothers were usually the more open and enthusiastic point of contact, but if the father did not want to join, the whole family often withdrew. Even after I reassured them that I could run the session with only the mother and children or other family members, there still seemed to be a clear sense that the father’s decision carried the most weight. Fortunately, in this case, he agreed to join in the end.

Observations

  • The conversation deck worked very well in this session, especially because it was structured over two rounds. During the first round, players were more reserved and still adjusting to the format, but by the second round they were much more comfortable and answered more openly.
  • The mother and daughter were the strongest storytellers in the session, often giving detailed responses and recalling additional memories as the conversation went on.
  • The mother seemed especially delighted to hear stories from her children that she would not normally get the chance to hear in everyday life.
  • The son felt comfortable enough to talk about his crushes and love interests, which the mother found very amusing, suggesting that the format created a softer and safer entry point into topics that might otherwise feel awkward.
  • The daughter reflected on her own experience of getting married and moving out, which was something her parents reportedly had never really asked her about before.
  • The children also reported learning stories from their parents that they would not otherwise have known or thought to ask about.
  • The father appeared pleased with the overall experience, even though he had initially been reluctant to participate.
  • The daughter who had recently married later expressed relief that her husband had not joined, as she felt his presence would have altered the dynamic of the whole session for everyone.
  • During the board game, the family was enthusiastic about the generation-specific mechanics, and all players intentionally picked generations different from their own.
  • The board game created a lighter and more playful atmosphere than the conversation deck, although it did not produce as many stories.
  • A pattern that emerged here, and later in other sessions as well, was that when the parents struggled with understanding the game rules, the children naturally stepped in to explain them and took over the role of facilitator.

Challenges & Reflections

This session was a strong reminder that recruitment itself is already part of the research insight. The repeated reluctance of fathers to participate suggests that intergenerational and familial communication activities are not entered into equally by all family members, and that patriarchal dynamics still shape who gets to decide whether these sessions happen at all. In this case, the father’s eventual participation seemed to contribute positively to the experience, but the hesitation leading up to the session was itself meaningful.

In terms of the prototypes, the session confirmed that the card deck is currently much more effective at drawing out stories than the board game. The two-round structure seems especially useful, because it allows players to warm up gradually rather than expecting deep conversation immediately. By contrast, the board game succeeded more in creating a light-hearted atmosphere and comparative discussion around generations, but it did not yet generate storytelling at the same depth. This suggests that the game still needs stronger narrative prompts or better-integrated communication mechanics if it is to function as more than a playful comparative simulation.

I was also interested in how naturally the children took on the role of facilitator when their parents became confused by the rules. Since I deliberately tried not to interfere once the session began, this showed that facilitation can emerge internally within the family when the structure allows for it. That is useful for my project, because one of the things I am hoping for is that the prototypes can eventually travel and function without me needing to be present all the time.

Next Steps

  • Keep the two-round structure of the card deck, as it appears to help families ease into deeper conversation.
  • Strengthen the storytelling component of the board game so that it can generate more meaningful exchanges rather than mainly light comparison.
  • Consider how family composition, including the presence of in-laws or spouses, changes the dynamic of participation and openness.
  • Reflect further on how facilitation roles shift within families, especially when younger members help older members navigate the game.
  • Continue documenting recruitment patterns, as reluctance to join may itself reveal existing familial communication structures.
