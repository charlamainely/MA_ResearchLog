Fieldwork

Investigating Interaction Designs at Singapore National Gallery

Date: 2025-08-30 Location: Singapore Method: Observation & Interaction

Context & Objective

Knowing that I want to design an interaction that helps translate expansive cultural content into a digestible and engaging experience, I wanted to explore if the exhibitions at Singapore Botanic Gardens have implemented any interactive design and explore how effective they are at supporting the exhibition content.

What Happened

During our walk around the Centre for Ethnobotany, I found a very beautiful interaction display called 'Burkill's Dictionary', which is a a digitized version of Burkill's Dictionary of the Economic Products of the Malay Peninsula in a space designed to resemble his study, allowing visitors to explore excerpts from the 1935 document on useful tropical plants and their indigenous knowledge.

With the heavy text content, the interaction was designed to resemble the physical book, and visitors can 'flip' the page to see the content, which is quite immersive. The entire interaction is designed very beautifully and looks like a dated manuscript, which is fitting with the theme and the spatial design of the building. However, aside from the flipping pages, I felt that the display doesn't employ any further interaction design and was still heavily reliant on long text content with occasional images.

The kiosk presents three branches; however, the choice labels are vague (e.g., “A path opens”). The curator wants more descriptive prompts and audio narration for non-readers.

Key Observations / Insights

  • Choice-based narratives increase dwell time for children; older audiences prioritize artifacts.
  • Branch labels are too abstract; descriptive prompts likely improve comprehension.
  • Audio narration and bigger type would broaden accessibility.
  • Folktale variants can be surfaced as “alternate paths” without overwhelming visitors.

Reflection

This session reinforces a dual-track presentation: concise artifact-first routes and deeper branching folktale paths for engaged visitors. For the web prototype, I’ll retain branching but clarify prompts and add optional audio. The folktale “variant” mechanic can house regional differences without feeling exhaustive.

Next Actions

  • Prototype clearer branch labels and prompt text; test with 3–5 users.
  • Add audio narration toggle and evaluate load time on mobile.
  • Map one folktale with 2–3 regional variants as optional branches.
