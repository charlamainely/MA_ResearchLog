Fieldwork

Research Residency at Tusitala Studio

Date: 2025-12-01 - 2025-12-12 Location: Tusitala Studio Method: Playtest Observation & Design Support

Summary

In this fieldwork opportunity, I joined Tusitala Studio as a playtest assistant for an upcoming immersive experience. My role involved supporting the playtest session itself, observing players during the experience, attending post-game interviews, reviewing the game and narrative design materials, and contributing feedback where relevant. I also worked on designing a documentation system that could help different departments record their work, reflections, and observations throughout the process. This experience offered a valuable behind-the-scenes look into how immersive narrative experiences are tested and refined, and how many different layers of design need to be coordinated in order for the final experience to feel coherent to players.

Observations

  • Playtesting is extremely important, especially in narrative and immersive experiences, because player responses are often surprising and difficult to predict in advance.
  • What players do during the experience and what they say afterward are not always the same, which makes both in-game observation and post-game interviews equally valuable.
  • Players tend to enjoy exploring narratives when there is an element of mystery, uncertainty, or a problem that they need to solve for themselves.
  • Every player approaches the experience differently, whether in terms of pacing, curiosity, interpretation, or willingness to engage with certain mechanics.
  • Designing an immersive in-person experience is especially challenging because narrative, spatial flow, interaction design, and player behaviour all affect each other at the same time.
  • Narrative elements in this kind of format need to remain cohesive and easy to digest, otherwise players can quickly become confused or disengaged.
  • Documentation is also an important part of the process, as different departments may notice different issues, and having a shared reflection system helps preserve these insights for later iterations.

Challenges & Reflections

One of the clearest insights from this experience was just how unpredictable players can be. Even when a design seems logical on paper, players may focus on unexpected details, miss information that the designers thought was obvious, or interpret mechanics in ways that were never intended. This reinforced for me that playtesting is not simply a final validation stage, but a central part of the design process itself. It is through testing that the actual experience of the player becomes visible, rather than the imagined experience held by the design team.

I was also struck by how different the insights from observation and interviews can be. During the game, players may seem confused, hesitant, or highly engaged, but their verbal reflections afterward can reveal motivations and interpretations that are not immediately visible from behaviour alone. On the other hand, players may not always be fully aware of what shaped their experience until prompted. This made me realise that relying on only one form of feedback would produce an incomplete picture. For immersive and narrative-driven experiences especially, both forms of feedback are necessary.

This fieldwork also made me appreciate how difficult it is to build a cohesive immersive experience in physical space. Unlike more contained games, this format requires narrative clarity, environmental design, interaction cues, and player flow to all work together at once. If one part fails, it can affect how the entire experience is understood. This connects closely to my own research, particularly in thinking about how players engage with designed experiences emotionally and socially, and how much iteration is needed before a system truly supports the intended outcome.

Next Steps

  • Continue thinking about how observation and interview methods can be paired more intentionally in my own playtesting and user research.
  • Consider how mystery, discovery, and problem-solving can be used more effectively to motivate engagement in my own prototypes.
  • Reflect on how narrative information can be structured so that it remains cohesive and legible even in more complex or multi-part experiences.
  • Explore better ways to document iterative design processes, especially when multiple collaborators or different design departments are involved.
  • Apply this reminder to my own work that player behaviour will always contain surprises, and that repeated testing is necessary rather than optional.
