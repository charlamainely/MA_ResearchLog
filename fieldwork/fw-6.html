Fieldwork

Hosting the Mekong Storyteller Panel Discussion and Short Film Screening

Date: 2025-11-08 & 2025-11-09 Location: LASALLE College of the Arts & Suntec Golden Village Method: Event Hosting, Interview & Observation

Summary

In this fieldwork activity, I supported the planning of the Mekong Storyteller panel discussion and short film screening, and also served as the event host for the panel and post-screening Q&A with the filmmakers. Through this role, I was able to speak directly with filmmakers from the Mekong region and gather insights into storytelling as both a creative practice and a cultural necessity. The event also provided a useful opportunity to observe what kinds of stories resonate most strongly with audiences, and how filmmakers approach the challenge of translating lived, local, and often intimate stories into visual form.

Observations

  • Many meaningful stories are embedded in everyday lives and ordinary storytellers, rather than only in grand historical or officially recognised narratives.
  • These everyday stories often reflect the complex historical, societal, and cultural conditions that people in the Mekong region live through.
  • Among the themes discussed and reflected in the screening, family and relationships appeared to be especially strong and popular with audiences.
  • Filmmakers play an important role in translating stories into visual form, but they also carry the responsibility of doing so with care and respect toward the original story and the people behind it.
  • Storytelling in this context is not only an artistic act, but also a way of preserving memory, expressing identity, and making lived experience visible to others.
  • The discussion suggested that audiences are highly receptive to stories that feel emotionally grounded and recognisable, especially when they involve family tensions, generational relationships, or intimate interpersonal dynamics.

Challenges & Reflections

One important reflection from this event is that powerful stories are often already present within communities, but they are not always immediately visible or easy to articulate. Everyday participants may have rich experiences and memories, but they may still need support in shaping those experiences into a form that can be shared. This is especially relevant to my own research, because it suggests that story collection is not just about asking people to “tell a story,” but about designing the right conditions, prompts, and scaffolds that help a story emerge.

I was also interested in the strong audience response to family and relationship-based themes. While large political or historical issues are certainly important, it seems that stories grounded in family life often create a more immediate emotional entry point for audiences. This matters for my project because it reinforces the idea that familial storytelling is not only personally significant, but also culturally resonant and publicly legible. There is clearly an appetite for stories that explore how people relate to one another across generations, obligations, misunderstandings, and care.

Another takeaway from the discussion is that storytelling is a form of translation. Filmmakers are not simply reproducing a story, but interpreting it into another medium while trying to remain faithful to its emotional and cultural truth. This connects closely to my own design process, where I am also trying to translate lived experiences and interpersonal dynamics into playable and participatory formats. It reminds me that translation always involves mediation, and that this mediation needs to be handled thoughtfully.

Next Steps

  • Continue exploring how prototypes can act as tools for drawing out existing stories in a self-facilitated or lightly facilitated way.
  • Develop better prompts and scaffolds that can help everyday participants construct or share stories more comfortably on the spot.
  • Investigate family and relationship-based themes more deeply, since they seem to resonate strongly with both participants and audiences.
  • Reflect on how stories can be translated into interactive or participatory formats while still respecting the integrity of the original experience.
  • Consider how public-facing events such as screenings and panel discussions can function as research spaces for identifying recurring themes, audience interests, and shared cultural concerns.
