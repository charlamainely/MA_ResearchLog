Fieldwork

Eason Yang Masterclass

Date: 2025-09-23 - 2025-09-25 Location: LASALLE College of the Arts Method: Workshop Participation & Observation

Summary

I attended this masterclass by Eason Yang after being offered by my supervisor to join a workshop organised for the Bachelor of Social Futures. In the session, I worked with a group of students to develop social innovation concepts for deaf users, drawing from interview insights gathered by the team. The workshop focused on how to translate user needs into scalable and potentially sustainable prototypes, while also encouraging us to think about long-term impact through frameworks such as Theory of Change. My contribution to the discussion centred mainly on proposing a gamified angle to the prototype and considering how playful interaction might help improve adoption and engagement.

Observations

  • The workshop placed strong emphasis on grounding ideas in real user interviews rather than jumping immediately into solution-making.
  • Designing for deaf users requires more specific consideration than simply “making something accessible,” as communication preferences, social context, and modes of interaction all shape how a solution is received.
  • The idea of scalability was important throughout the session, suggesting that social innovation projects are often evaluated not only by empathy or creativity, but also by whether they can be sustained, expanded, or realistically implemented.
  • Theory of Change was introduced as a useful framework for linking small design interventions to broader desired outcomes, helping teams think more clearly about how and why a prototype is meant to create impact.
  • Rapid idea testing was presented as an important part of the process, reinforcing that early concepts should be challenged and iterated quickly rather than protected too carefully.
  • I found that proposing a gamified angle opened up useful discussion around motivation, habit formation, and user adoption, especially when the prototype needed to encourage repeated use rather than one-time participation.

Challenges & Reflections

One of the more useful tensions in this workshop was the challenge of balancing social impact with practicality. It is easy to generate ideas that sound meaningful in principle, but much harder to shape them into something that users would actually adopt, that stakeholders could support, and that might eventually grow beyond a one-off prototype. The workshop therefore encouraged a mode of thinking that was not only human-centred, but also systems-aware. This was helpful for me because it pushed the conversation beyond “what is a good idea?” into “what kind of structure would allow this idea to survive and matter?”

I was also reminded that gamification should not be treated as decoration. If introduced carelessly, it can feel superficial or manipulative. But when used thoughtfully, it can become a meaningful way to encourage repeated engagement, lower participation barriers, and make a tool feel less intimidating. This connects closely to my own research, since I am also interested in how playful systems can support emotionally or socially difficult forms of participation. The workshop reinforced that the value of a gamified element lies not in novelty, but in whether it genuinely helps the user stay engaged with the intended behaviour or experience.

Another useful takeaway was the relationship between research and prototyping. Because the team had interview insights to work from, the design process felt more anchored, even when the ideas were still rough. This reminded me that strong prototypes often emerge not from raw creativity alone, but from carefully interpreting what people actually need, struggle with, or desire. In that sense, the workshop was a good reminder that ideation works best when it remains closely tied to evidence and reflection.

Next Steps

  • Think more carefully about how user interview insights can be translated into design criteria rather than just general inspiration.
  • Continue exploring how playful or gamified elements can support adoption, especially in socially meaningful or behaviour-changing prototypes.
  • Look further into Theory of Change as a framework for mapping how a prototype might lead to broader social or behavioural outcomes.
  • Reflect on the tension between designing for empathy and designing for long-term feasibility, especially when working on socially oriented projects.
  • Consider how rapid testing methods can be used earlier in my own process to challenge assumptions before becoming too attached to one direction.
