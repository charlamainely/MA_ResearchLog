Fieldwork

Volunteering at the Don Norman Design Award 2025

Date: 2025-11-19 - 2025-11-21 Location: Singapore Polytechnic Method: Conference Participation, Facilitation & Observation

Summary

I volunteered at the Don Norman Design Award 2025 as an unconference facilitator, which meant helping to support sessions where participants were invited to facilitate their own discussions rather than follow a fixed panel format. Although the role felt slightly unusual at first, it ended up being a very enjoyable way to observe how design conversations emerge when people are given more ownership over the exchange. Through the event, I was also able to sit in on talks by designers and thinkers from Singapore and around the world, including meeting Don Norman himself and Ezio Manzini. A major theme that ran through the conference was the shift from conventional human-centred design toward a broader humanity-centred and systems-aware approach, especially when addressing social and environmental challenges.

Observations

  • A recurring idea throughout the conference was that design should move beyond simply “solving problems” and instead focus on designing the conditions for meaningful change, especially by identifying root causes within larger systems.
  • Participatory and community-led design was framed as essential rather than optional when the goal is lasting social impact. Communities are unlikely to accept change unless they have had real agency in shaping it.
  • Don Norman’s discussion of humanity-centred design expanded the usual user-centred lens by asking designers to consider wider societal, systemic, and environmental consequences rather than isolated user needs alone.
  • Play, storytelling, and beginner’s mindset were discussed as valuable tools for empathy, learning, and reframing narratives, especially when trying to engage people with difficult, abstract, or entrenched issues.
  • Several talks emphasised that designers are not experts in everything, and that this can actually be a strength. Not knowing too much can make it easier to ask better questions, collaborate openly, and remain receptive to alternative possibilities.
  • Another strong theme was the importance of designing for networks and relationships rather than isolated touchpoints, especially when trying to scale social innovation or community-based work.
  • Co-creation, small tests, and iterative pilot processes were presented as more effective than trying to impose a finished solution from the top down.
  • The unconference format itself reflected these values, as it created room for open discussion, shared authorship, and more distributed participation rather than fixed one-way expertise.

Challenges & Reflections

One of the most useful ideas I took from this event was the shift from seeing design as problem-solving toward seeing design as a way of reshaping conditions, relationships, and systems. This feels especially relevant to my own research, because I am not really trying to “fix” family communication in a simple or totalising way. Instead, I am more interested in how design might create the conditions for reflection, empathy, and more constructive interaction. Hearing this articulated in a broader design context helped me better situate my own work.

I was also struck by how often play and storytelling appeared, not as trivial add-ons, but as serious methods for learning, reframing, and opening people up to new ways of seeing. This strongly validates the direction of my own practice. If play can help people approach difficult social issues, and storytelling can make abstract systems feel emotionally legible, then these are not just aesthetic preferences in my work but meaningful methodological tools. The conference helped me see these methods as part of a larger design discourse rather than as isolated personal interests.

Another important reflection came from the discussions around beginner’s mindset and collaboration. The idea that designers do not need to know everything, and perhaps should not assume that they do, was quite refreshing. It reinforced the importance of asking good questions, working across disciplines, and allowing communities and participants to influence the direction of a project. This also connects to the unconference format itself, which felt like a practical demonstration of how conversation can be designed more openly. Rather than controlling every outcome, facilitation in this context was about creating the right conditions for people to think together.

Next Steps

  • Reflect more critically on how my own research can be framed not as solving a social problem, but as designing conditions that support better relational possibilities.
  • Continue exploring participatory and community-led methods as non-negotiable parts of my design process, especially when working with lived experience and storytelling.
  • Use play, storytelling, and narrative reframing more intentionally as tools for empathy and engagement rather than only as formats for presentation.
  • Think further about how prototypes might operate at the level of networks and relationships, not just individual interactions.
  • Hold onto beginner’s mindset as a useful design stance, especially when entering unfamiliar social contexts or interdisciplinary collaborations.
