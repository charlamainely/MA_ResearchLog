Site Visit to Dover Forest with the Mixed Reality Lab

Date: 2025-12-05 Location: Dover Forest, Singapore Method: Site Visit & 360 Capture Observation

Summary

One of the most memorable days during my residency was joining the team on a site visit to Dover Forest, where we carried 360 camera equipment into the forest in order to capture immersive footage of the site before its anticipated demolition. This visit gave me a much more direct understanding of what virtual heritage work can look like in practice: not just sitting in a lab with technology, but physically going into a site, documenting it carefully, and thinking about what it means to preserve a place that may soon disappear.

Observations

  • The purpose of the visit was to capture the forest in 360 so that it could still be explored in immersive form in the future, even if the physical site is later destroyed.
  • This fieldwork highlighted that virtual heritage can apply not only to buildings or monuments, but also to natural environments that hold ecological, cultural, or emotional significance.
  • The use of 360 cameras allows for a more complete visual record of a place, preserving not just isolated views but a fuller sense of spatial immersion.
  • Being physically present in the forest changed my understanding of the site. It was not just a location to be documented, but a living environment with atmosphere, density, and a sense of scale that is difficult to communicate through conventional media.
  • I was struck by the fact that such a wild, seemingly untamed piece of land could exist in the middle of Singapore, which made the possibility of its disappearance feel even more significant.
  • The experience also made it clear that while immersive capture can preserve visual access, it cannot fully reproduce the embodied feeling of moving through the actual site.

Challenges & Reflections

This site visit made the limitations of preservation very tangible to me. The technology can capture a remarkable amount of visual detail and spatial continuity, but being there in person is still a very different experience. What stayed with me most was not only the image of the forest, but the feeling of entering it: the surprise of finding such a dense natural environment in Singapore, the effort of carrying equipment through it, and the awareness that this place might not remain in the form I saw it. Those dimensions are much harder to preserve.

It also reinforced my growing belief that visual accuracy alone is not enough. Without some kind of storytelling or interpretive frame, preserved footage can become a technically impressive but emotionally flat record. The forest mattered to me precisely because I knew why we were there, what might happen to it, and what this act of capture was trying to resist. That context created the emotional charge. In other words, it was not only the place itself, but the story around the place, that made it meaningful.

This insight is very relevant to my own research. Whether I am working with family memories, reflective play, or forms of story capture, the challenge is similar: how do we create not only a record, but also a meaningful entry point for others to care? This site visit made me think more deeply about how design can help bridge that gap between preserved material and lived emotional connection.

Next Steps

  • Reflect more deeply on how place-based experiences might be translated into meaningful narrative or interactive formats.
  • Consider how context and storytelling shape whether preserved materials feel emotionally resonant or merely informative.
  • Explore how immersive capture technologies preserve presence, and where they still fall short compared to embodied experience.
  • Think about how my own work might preserve not only stories themselves, but also some sense of their emotional and situational context.
  • Continue investigating how threatened or disappearing sites can be documented in ways that invite care rather than passive viewing.
