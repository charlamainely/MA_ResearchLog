User Testing Session 2 - Family with Two Sons

Date: [Insert date] Location: [Insert location] Method: Prototype User Testing & Observation

Summary

For the second user testing session, I worked with a family made up of a father, a mother, and two sons, one in high school and one in university. In structure, the family looked quite similar to the first session, but in terms of conversational dynamic they were actually very different. This family reportedly already communicated quite openly and casually with one another, so the session revealed less of a need to “break the ice” and more of a need to direct and shape existing communication into something more focused.

Observations

  • The family had a good time with the conversation deck and appeared comfortable speaking with one another from the first round, unlike the previous family who needed time to warm up.
  • This group gave a lot of useful feedback about the questions themselves, especially from the younger children, who pointed out that some prompts require more life experience in order to compare the past to the present in a meaningful way.
  • This suggests that some of the prompts in the deck were still too biased toward older players and needed revision in order to be more inclusive.
  • The family also pointed out the need for stronger “safety rails” or conversational boundaries to help players be more aware of how long they were speaking and whether they were drifting off-topic.
  • Even though they already shared stories casually in everyday life, the prompts still helped direct the conversation in ways that allowed new stories to emerge.
  • This group swapped out cards far more frequently than the previous family, suggesting that more cards were needed in the deck overall to support variety and maintain interest.
  • During the board game, the group enjoyed seeing the generation-specific stat effects and comparing differences across players, but they found the question mechanic too overloaded and suggested removing it.
  • As in the previous session, all players chose generations different from their own, which seems to suggest that the generational role-play mechanic is inherently appealing.
  • Another noticeable pattern was that family members often chose very similar life paths to each other once one player made a choice, especially when a parent picked a certain educational or life route first. Only the youngest player chose a significantly different path.
  • The youngest player expressed interest in seeing more mechanics that allow the different generations to interact with one another more directly during the game.

Challenges & Reflections

What was especially useful about this session is that it showed me how the prototypes behave in a family that is already relatively communicative and close. This is important because it means the prototypes are not only useful in families that are tense or distant, but can also work as structures that direct already existing openness into new topics. In this case, the conversation deck did not so much “unlock” communication as give it shape and focus.

The feedback about the questions was particularly valuable. Since younger participants pointed out that some prompts assume a level of life experience they simply do not have yet, this made it clear that the deck still needed to be revised to better accommodate different age groups. This is a very relevant issue for my project, since intergenerational play cannot work well if one generation consistently feels excluded from the prompts themselves.

In contrast, the board game again showed that while the generational comparison mechanic is enjoyable, the added question mechanic may currently be too much. The fact that players liked the stat differences but found the question layer overloaded suggests that I need to be much more careful about where and how I insert storytelling into the game. At the moment, too many demands at once may be reducing the overall clarity of the experience.

I was also intrigued by the tendency of the family to choose similar life paths to each other in the game. This suggests that player choice is not only individual, but socially influenced in the moment. It may indicate conformity, imitation, or a desire to compare like-with-like, all of which are interesting dynamics to think about in later iterations.

Next Steps

  • Revise the card deck so that younger players are not excluded by prompts that assume too much life experience.
  • Expand the number of cards in the deck, since players are currently cycling through and replacing them too quickly.
  • Consider implementing clearer conversational boundaries or facilitation cues to help keep stories focused without becoming overly restrictive.
  • Reconsider the board game question mechanic, as it currently appears too overloaded for smooth play.
  • Explore ways for different generations to interact more directly with one another inside the board game itself, since this was explicitly requested by the youngest player.
