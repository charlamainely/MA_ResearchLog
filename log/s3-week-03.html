Week 3 - Content Explosion

Dates: 2026-06-01 → 2026-06-07

Activities

  • Organise growing board game content in spreadsheet format
  • Continue recruiting families in Vietnam for testing
  • Turn story archive ideas into a functioning website and purchase domain

Reflection

This week the content of the board game really started to grow out of hand. Not only was I introducing a question mechanic that would require players to answer prompts for each space, but I also decided that each space should now have different descriptions for each generation. Previously, the spaces only had different stat effects, but now even the narrative framing of the same space would change depending on whether the player is representing an older or younger generation. This made the game much richer, but also much more difficult to manage manually.

So I ended up setting up a spreadsheet to keep track of all the text and data in one place, which can then be pumped back into the game and used to update the placeholder text fields more systematically. This was honestly very necessary, because without some kind of content management system, I would very quickly lose track of which generation-specific text belongs where. It also made me realise that my board game is not only becoming a design object, but also a small content system that needs proper organisation.

In terms of family recruitment, I got some confirmations from families in Vietnam, though most were still understandably hesitant about the content of my project and what would be required from them. Most of the concern was really about the logistics of gathering the whole family in one place, which is already a challenge on its own, and then having to sit down and talk to each other, which is ironically exactly the point of my research. Some of the older participants were also nervous when I mentioned playing games together, because they worried they would not understand the rules and mechanics and might be mocked by their children. I gave them assurance that the games are meant to be simple and that I would facilitate carefully, but this was still an important reminder of how emotionally loaded even the invitation to play can be.

I also turned the story archive ideas into a functioning website this week, and even bought a domain for it. This felt like a nice milestone, because the archive stopped being just an idea and became something I could actually see and interact with. I am still working out how to properly connect the code to the domain, but at least the structure and interface of the site now exist and give me something concrete to keep building from.
