Reflection

After reviewing in-class activities and feedback from last week, it was clear that using browser-based games to engage with the community for the purpose of intangible cultural heritage (ICH) preservation is a potential direction, but the scope is too big. I should start from a specific example to test feasibility, then derive a framework to apply to similar ICH categories. However, aside from legal definitions in recent Vietnamese cultural-preservation policy, there’s no single comprehensive public database of what is (or could be) recognized as ICH.

While choosing an example, a conversation with a screenwriter/producer acquaintance surfaced the theme of preserving the art of storytelling—pointing me toward oral traditions, not only literary heritage.

Vietnam has UNESCO-recognized oral traditions, but narratives (specifically folktales) are less preserved as living practice. Folktales are well documented in textbooks; yet the way they’re inscribed/taught makes them static and simplified, reducing opportunities for living variation and the embedded lessons carried by retellings.

At a meso level, a ground-up, community approach can complement existing ICH efforts and directly engage digital natives. At a macro level, “gamifying” folktales may facilitate active storytelling practices in digital spaces. Read my in-depth MMM analysis here.
